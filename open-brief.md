# Open brief

The four numbered challenges in this repository are examples. They are provided to give teams a well-defined problem with starter data and hints. You are not required to use them.

If your team has a problem from your own work that you would rather tackle, bring it. Teams are actively encouraged to do this. Some of the most useful things built at events like this are solutions to problems people have been tolerating for years.

## What makes a good open brief problem?

A good problem has three things:

**A real user** — someone who is experiencing something difficult today. Not a hypothetical user, but a person (or type of person) whose working day or interaction with government is worse than it needs to be. The more specifically you can describe them, the better your prototype will be.

**A clear gap** — between the experience that person has today and what a better version would look like. The gap does not need to be large. A small gap that affects a lot of people, or that causes a specific and significant pain, is enough.

**A prototype you can demo** — something a judge can see working by 15:00. It does not need to be complete or production-ready. It needs to demonstrate the idea convincingly enough that someone can understand the value of the full thing.

## What to do on the day

Speak to a facilitator during the morning session — before 10:00. Briefly describe your problem, who it affects, and what you think a prototype could demonstrate. A facilitator or your FDE will help you assess whether it is scoped for one day.

Your problem must use open or synthetic data. Do not use live production data, personal data, or anything you are not cleared to handle outside your usual systems.

If you are unsure whether your idea is suitable, speak to your FDE before the morning session closes. It is better to have that conversation early than to discover a problem with your approach at lunchtime.

## Who you are building for

Before you start building, be specific about your users. Open brief teams sometimes run into difficulty because the problem is framed around a system or a process rather than a person. Ask:

- Who is the primary user — the person who would use this prototype directly?
- What is their role, and what does their day look like?
- What specific task or interaction is painful for them today?
- What would they notice if it were better?
- Is there a secondary user — someone else who is affected by the same process?

The clearer your answer to these questions, the better your demo will be. A prototype that solves one specific problem for one specific person is more convincing than one that addresses a broad challenge without a clear user in mind.

## Prompts to help you frame your problem

Use these with your AI coding tool before or during the morning planning session.

**Framing the problem:**

```
I work in [describe your role or team]. One process that creates
friction for people is [describe the process]. Help me frame
this as a problem statement: who is affected, what is their
current experience, what would a better experience look like,
and what could a one-day prototype realistically demonstrate?
```

**Identifying the users:**

```
The problem I want to tackle is [describe it briefly]. Help me
think about who the users are. Who is directly affected? What
does their day look like? What specific moment in their
experience is most painful? Is there anyone else affected
indirectly?
```

**Scoping for one day:**

```
I want to build a prototype for [describe the problem] in one
day. What is the smallest useful version of this? What would
it need to do to be a convincing demo? What could I leave out
without losing the core of the idea?
```

**Finding or generating data:**

```
My prototype needs data about [describe it]. What data might
already be publicly available that I could use? If I need to
generate synthetic data, what fields and structure would make
sense? Generate an example dataset I could start with.
```

**Checking your idea:**

```
Here is my problem statement: [paste it]. What could go wrong
with this as a one-day hackathon challenge? Is the scope
realistic? Are there any assumptions I am making that I should
test before I start building?
```

## What does a good outcome look like?

By the end of the day, you should be able to show a working prototype and explain clearly: who it is for, what problem it solves, and why the current situation is worse than what you built. You should also be able to say honestly what is missing, what assumptions you made, and what you would do next with more time.

Judges will not penalise you for an ambitious problem that you did not fully solve. They will penalise you for a prototype that does not have a clear user or a clear problem behind it.
