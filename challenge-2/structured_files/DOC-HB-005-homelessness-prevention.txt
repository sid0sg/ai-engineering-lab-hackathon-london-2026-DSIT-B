Homelessness Prevention and Relief: Local Authority Duties and Assessment Procedures
Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities
Published: January 2025
Document ID: DOC-HB-005


LOCAL AUTHORITY DUTIES UNDER HOMELESSNESS LEGISLATION

Local authorities in England have a range of statutory duties towards people who are homeless or threatened with homelessness. These duties are principally contained in Part 7 of the Housing Act 1996, as significantly amended by the Homelessness Reduction Act 2017. The 2017 Act introduced new prevention and relief duties that substantially expanded the obligations placed on local authorities and extended the period during which a person is treated as threatened with homelessness from 28 days to 56 days before the likely loss of accommodation. When a person approaches a local authority for assistance because they are homeless or at risk of becoming homeless, the authority must carry out an assessment of their circumstances and their housing and support needs. This initial assessment is a critical stage in the process and must be conducted in a manner that is thorough, sensitive, and compliant with the requirements set out in the legislation and the accompanying statutory code of guidance. The assessment should consider the full range of the applicant's circumstances, including the reasons for their homelessness or threatened homelessness, their housing history, their financial situation including any entitlement to Housing Benefit as set out in the Housing Benefit eligibility guidance (DOC-HB-001), their health and wellbeing, any support needs they may have, and whether they have dependent children or other members of their household who must be accommodated with them. Authorities should ensure that assessments are conducted by appropriately trained staff and that interpreting services are made available where the applicant's first language is not English. The outcome of the assessment must be recorded in writing and a copy provided to the applicant in the form of a personalised housing plan, which sets out the steps that both the authority and the applicant will take to try to prevent or relieve their homelessness.

The prevention duty arises under section 195 of the Housing Act 1996 where the authority is satisfied that the applicant is threatened with homelessness and is eligible for assistance. The authority must take reasonable steps to help the applicant secure that accommodation does not cease to be available for their occupation. Reasonable steps may include negotiation with landlords to prevent eviction, assistance with rent arrears or deposits through Discretionary Housing Payments as described in the guidance for local authorities (DOC-HB-002), referral to debt advice or mediation services, or assistance to secure alternative accommodation in the private or social rented sector. The prevention duty lasts for 56 days or until it is brought to an end by one of the circumstances specified in the Act, for example where the applicant becomes homeless, where suitable accommodation is secured for at least six months, or where the applicant deliberately and unreasonably refuses to cooperate with the steps set out in the personalised housing plan. Authorities are expected to engage actively with applicants during the prevention duty period and to take a proactive rather than reactive approach. Where the authority becomes aware that an applicant's circumstances have changed, for example because an eviction date has been brought forward or because the applicant's health has deteriorated, the personalised housing plan should be reviewed and updated accordingly. The effectiveness of prevention work is monitored through the Homelessness Case Level Information Collection (H-CLIC) data returns that authorities are required to submit to the Department on a quarterly basis, and authorities are encouraged to use this data to identify trends and improve their prevention services.

Where homelessness is not prevented and the applicant becomes homeless, the relief duty under section 189B of the Housing Act 1996 is engaged. The authority must take reasonable steps to help the applicant secure suitable accommodation. This may involve assisting the applicant to find accommodation in the private rented sector, making a direct offer of temporary accommodation, or facilitating access to supported housing or social housing through the council's allocation scheme, the details of which are commonly asked about in the social housing frequently asked questions (DOC-HB-004). The relief duty also lasts for 56 days and is subject to similar provisions regarding cooperation and the circumstances in which it may come to an end. During the relief duty period, the authority must also consider whether it has a duty to provide interim accommodation under section 188 of the Act. This duty arises where the authority has reason to believe that the applicant may be homeless, eligible for assistance, and in priority need. Priority need categories include households with dependent children, pregnant women, persons who are vulnerable as a result of old age, mental illness, physical disability, or other special reason, and persons who are homeless as a result of an emergency such as a fire or flood. The standard of temporary accommodation provided must be suitable for the applicant and their household, and the suitability of accommodation is assessed by reference to factors including location, affordability, condition, and the needs of the household.

If the relief duty comes to an end without the applicant having secured accommodation, the authority must then consider whether it owes the main housing duty under section 193 of the Housing Act 1996. The main duty is owed where the applicant is homeless, eligible for assistance, in priority need, and not intentionally homeless. Where the main duty is accepted, the authority must secure that suitable accommodation is available for the applicant until the duty comes to an end, which typically occurs when the applicant accepts an offer of settled accommodation through the allocation scheme or accepts a qualifying offer of an assured shorthold tenancy in the private rented sector. The main duty represents the final safety net and authorities should ensure that applicants who are owed this duty are provided with accommodation that meets their needs and that active efforts continue to help them secure long-term settled housing. Authorities should also be mindful of the duty to refer introduced by section 213B of the Housing Act 1996, which requires specified public authorities including hospitals, prisons, jobcentres, and social services departments to refer service users who they have reason to believe may be homeless or threatened with homelessness to the local housing authority with the individual's consent. This duty to refer is intended to ensure that people at risk of homelessness are identified early and connected with housing assistance before they reach crisis point, and it requires effective information-sharing protocols between the referring authority and the housing authority. Further guidance on how the duty to refer interacts with welfare benefits support is set out in the state benefits coordination guidance (DOC-SB-008).
