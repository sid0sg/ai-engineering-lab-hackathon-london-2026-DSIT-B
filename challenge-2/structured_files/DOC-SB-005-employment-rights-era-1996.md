# Employment Rights Act 1996: Plain-English Summary

This document provides a plain-English summary of the key provisions of the Employment Rights Act 1996 (ERA 1996). It is intended to help employers, employees, and advisers understand the main employment rights established by the Act without needing to read the full legislative text. This summary does not constitute legal advice. Where specific questions arise, you should consult the Act itself or seek qualified legal counsel.

## 1. Right not to be unfairly dismissed

### 1.1 The basic right

Under Section 94(1) of the Employment Rights Act 1996, an employee who has been continuously employed for at least two years has the right not to be unfairly dismissed by their employer. This is one of the most important protections in UK employment law and applies to employees (but not to workers or self-employed contractors who do not have employee status).

### 1.2 Fair reasons for dismissal

Under Section 98(2), a dismissal is potentially fair only if it is for one of the following reasons: capability or qualifications, conduct, redundancy, contravention of a statutory duty or restriction, or some other substantial reason justifying the dismissal. The employer bears the burden of showing the reason for dismissal. Once the reason is established, an employment tribunal will assess whether the employer acted reasonably in treating that reason as sufficient for dismissal, having regard to the size and administrative resources of the employer and to equity and the substantial merits of the case.

### 1.3 Automatically unfair reasons

Certain reasons for dismissal are automatically unfair regardless of the length of the employee's service. Under various sections of the Act and related legislation, these include dismissal for asserting a statutory right under Section 104, dismissal connected with pregnancy or maternity under Section 99, dismissal for making a protected disclosure (whistleblowing) under Section 103A, and dismissal for a reason connected with trade union membership or activities.

## 2. Redundancy

### 2.1 Right to a redundancy payment

Under Section 135 of the Act, an employee who has been continuously employed for two years or more and who is dismissed by reason of redundancy is entitled to a statutory redundancy payment. The amount is calculated based on the employee's age, length of service (up to 20 years), and weekly pay (subject to a statutory cap, which is reviewed annually).

### 2.2 Consultation obligations

Although the detailed requirements for collective redundancy consultation are set out in the Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992 rather than the ERA 1996, employers should be aware that where 20 or more redundancies are proposed at a single establishment within a period of 90 days, formal consultation with employee representatives must take place. Failure to consult can result in a protective award of up to 90 days' pay per affected employee.

## 3. Flexible working

### 3.1 Right to request flexible working

Under Section 80F of the Act (as amended), all employees have the right to request a change to their terms and conditions relating to hours, times, or place of work from the first day of their employment. The employer must deal with the request in a reasonable manner and must notify the employee of the decision within two months of the request (unless an extension is agreed). An employer may only refuse a request on one of the business grounds specified in Section 80G, which include the burden of additional costs, a detrimental effect on the ability to meet customer demand, an inability to reorganise work among existing staff, and a detrimental impact on quality or performance.

### 3.2 Remedies

If an employer fails to deal with a request properly, the employee may bring a complaint to an employment tribunal. The tribunal may order the employer to reconsider the request and may award compensation of up to eight weeks' pay.

## 4. Notice periods

### 4.1 Minimum statutory notice

Under Section 86 of the Act, both employers and employees are required to give minimum periods of notice to terminate employment. An employer must give at least one week's notice for each year of continuous employment, up to a maximum of 12 weeks. An employee who has been employed for one month or more must give at least one week's notice. Contractual notice periods may exceed these minimums but cannot fall below them.

### 4.2 Payment in lieu of notice

An employer may make a payment in lieu of notice (PILON) instead of requiring the employee to work their notice period, but only where the contract expressly permits this or where both parties agree. A PILON is subject to Income Tax and National Insurance contributions in the normal way.

## 5. Written statement of employment particulars

Under Section 1 of the Act, an employer must provide every employee and worker with a written statement of employment particulars on or before the first day of employment. The statement must include the names of the parties, the date employment began, the rate and intervals of pay, hours of work, holiday entitlement, the job title or a description of the work, and the place of work. Failure to provide the statement may result in a tribunal awarding two or four weeks' pay in any subsequent tribunal claim.
