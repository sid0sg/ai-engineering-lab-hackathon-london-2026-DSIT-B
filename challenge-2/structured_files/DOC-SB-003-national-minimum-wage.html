National Minimum Wage and National Living Wage: Employer Guidance

Understand the minimum hourly pay rates you must offer your workers, how to check you are paying correctly, and what happens if you do not comply.

Current rates from April 2025

The following rates apply from 1 April 2025. All employers must pay at least these rates to qualifying workers. The National Living Wage applies to workers aged 21 and over. The National Minimum Wage applies to workers below that age and to apprentices.

Age Group Hourly Rate
21 and over (National Living Wage) 12.21 pounds
18 to 20 10.00 pounds
Under 18 7.55 pounds
Apprentice 7.55 pounds

The apprentice rate applies to apprentices aged under 19, or those aged 19 and over who are in the first year of their apprenticeship. All other apprentices are entitled to the rate for their age group.

Who is entitled to the minimum wage

Most workers in the UK are entitled to be paid at least the National Minimum Wage or National Living Wage. This includes part-time workers, agency workers, workers on zero-hours contracts, and foreign workers legally entitled to work in the UK. Some categories of worker are exempt, including volunteers, members of the armed forces, share fishermen, and prisoners. Self-employed individuals are not entitled to the minimum wage, but employers must ensure that individuals they treat as self-employed genuinely meet the legal definition of self-employment.

Common errors in calculating minimum wage pay

HMRC regularly investigates employers who underpay their workers. The following are the most frequently identified errors.

  1. Deducting costs for uniforms or equipment. If you require workers to purchase a uniform or specific tools, and those deductions bring their effective hourly pay below the minimum wage, you are in breach of the law. The deduction itself may be lawful, but the resulting pay must still meet the minimum rate.
  2. Not paying for all working time. Time spent travelling between assignments, mandatory training, time spent opening or closing premises, and time when a worker is required to be on the employer's premises and available for work all count as working time. Failing to pay for these hours is one of the most common sources of underpayment.
  3. Applying the wrong age band. A worker's rate must increase on their birthday when they move into a higher age band. Employers must have processes to ensure rate changes are applied from the correct pay reference period.
  4. Averaging pay incorrectly over the pay reference period. The minimum wage is assessed per pay reference period, which is normally no longer than one month. Averaging a worker's pay over a longer period to mask short-term underpayment is not permitted.
  5. Misclassifying salaried workers. Salaried workers on an annual salary can be paid the minimum wage differently from hourly-paid workers, but only if their contract meets specific conditions. If it does not, each pay reference period must be assessed individually.

Enforcement and penalties

HMRC enforces minimum wage legislation. If you are found to have underpaid workers, you will be required to pay all arrears owed and may face a financial penalty of up to 200 percent of the total underpayment, up to a maximum of 20,000 pounds per worker. Employers who repeatedly or deliberately fail to pay the minimum wage may also be publicly named by the government, prosecuted, and disqualified from acting as a company director for up to 15 years.
