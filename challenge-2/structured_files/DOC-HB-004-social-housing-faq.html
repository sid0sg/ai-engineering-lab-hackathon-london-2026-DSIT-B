Social Housing: Frequently Asked Questions

Last updated 10 August 2025
From: Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities

This page answers common questions about applying for and living in social housing in England. Social housing includes homes owned and managed by local councils and by housing associations (also known as registered providers of social housing).

Your questions answered

1. What is social housing and who provides it?

Social housing is affordable rented accommodation provided by local councils or housing associations. Rents in social housing are set significantly below market levels and are regulated by the government. Local councils own and manage council housing directly, while housing associations are independent not-for-profit organisations registered with the Regulator of Social Housing. Both types of social housing are allocated through your local council's housing register or waiting list.

2. How do I apply for social housing?

To apply for social housing you need to join your local council's housing register, sometimes called the waiting list. You can usually apply online through your council's website, or by contacting the council's housing options team by telephone or in person. You will need to provide information about your current living situation, your household, your income, and any particular housing needs you may have. Some councils operate a choice-based lettings system where you bid for available properties once you are on the register.

3. Who is eligible to go on the housing register?

Most people aged 18 or over can apply to join their council's housing register. However, certain groups are excluded by law, including:

  • people from abroad who are subject to immigration control and do not have leave to remain that entitles them to public funds
  • people who are not habitually resident in the United Kingdom, the Channel Islands, the Isle of Man, or the Republic of Ireland

Councils may also set their own qualification criteria, such as a local connection requirement. If you are found not to qualify, the council must give you written reasons and you have the right to request a review of that decision.

4. How are social housing properties allocated?

Councils must have an allocation scheme that sets out how they prioritise applicants. By law, reasonable preference must be given to certain groups, including:

  • people who are homeless or threatened with homelessness (see DOC-HB-005 for more detail on homelessness duties)
  • people living in unsanitary, overcrowded, or otherwise unsatisfactory housing
  • people who need to move on medical or welfare grounds
  • people who need to move to a particular area to avoid hardship

Within these groups, the council applies a banding or points system to determine the relative priority of applicants. The exact criteria differ between councils.

5. How long will I have to wait for social housing?

Waiting times vary considerably depending on your area, the type of property you need, and your priority band. In areas of high demand, particularly in London and the South East, waiting times of several years are common for general needs applicants. Applicants with urgent housing needs, such as those who are homeless or living in severely overcrowded conditions, are typically housed more quickly. Your council can give you an indication of likely waiting times in your area and band.

6. Can I choose where I live?

Many councils operate choice-based lettings systems that allow you to express a preference for available properties by placing a bid. You can usually bid for properties in areas you are interested in, subject to any restrictions on property size based on your household. However, there is no guarantee that you will be offered a property in your preferred area, particularly if demand is high. You are entitled to express a preference, but the council is not bound to allocate in accordance with that preference if doing so would not be consistent with the allocation scheme.

7. What size property am I entitled to?

The size of property you are eligible for is determined by the bedroom standard, which takes into account the number and ages of people in your household. The general rules are:

  • one bedroom for each adult couple
  • one bedroom for each other person aged 16 or over
  • one bedroom for two children of the same sex under 16
  • one bedroom for two children under 10 regardless of sex
  • one bedroom for any remaining child

An additional bedroom may be allowed for a carer who does not live with you but provides overnight care, or for a disabled person who cannot share a bedroom with their partner due to their disability.

8. Can I get help with rent in social housing?

Yes. If you are on a low income, you may be entitled to Housing Benefit or the housing costs element of Universal Credit to help with your rent. The eligibility rules and income thresholds for Housing Benefit are set out in the Housing Benefit eligibility guidance (DOC-HB-001). Many social housing tenants are entitled to the full amount of their rent through benefits because social rents are set at affordable levels.

9. What is the difference between a secure tenancy and an assured tenancy?

If you rent from a local council, you will usually have a secure tenancy under the Housing Act 1985. If you rent from a housing association, you will usually have an assured tenancy under the Housing Act 1988. Both types of tenancy give you strong security of tenure, meaning your landlord cannot evict you without a court order and a valid ground for possession. Some newer tenancies may be fixed-term rather than lifetime, particularly those granted under the flexible tenancy or assured shorthold tenancy provisions.

10. Can I transfer to a different social housing property?

If you are already a social housing tenant and want to move to a different property, you can apply for a transfer through your council's allocation scheme. Transfer applicants are assessed in the same way as new applicants, although some councils give additional priority to tenants who are under-occupying their current home and willing to move to a smaller property. You can also arrange a mutual exchange with another social housing tenant, which allows you to swap homes. Mutual exchanges must be approved by both landlords.

11. What if I am refused social housing or unhappy with a decision?

If your application to join the housing register is refused, or if you disagree with the priority band you have been placed in, you have the right to request a review. The council must carry out the review and notify you of the outcome with reasons. If you remain dissatisfied after the review, you may be able to make a complaint through the council's complaints procedure or, ultimately, to the Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman.

12. What rights do I have as a social housing tenant?

As a social housing tenant, you have a number of important rights, including:

  • the right to live in the property undisturbed, provided you comply with the tenancy conditions
  • the right to have repairs carried out within reasonable timescales
  • the right to be consulted about changes to the management of your home or estate
  • the right to take in lodgers or, with permission, sublet part of the property
  • the right to buy your home at a discount (for most secure council tenants under the Right to Buy scheme)
  • the right to make complaints and have them dealt with fairly

Your landlord must provide you with a written tenancy agreement setting out both your rights and responsibilities. If you believe your landlord has failed to meet their obligations, you can raise the matter with the Housing Ombudsman.
